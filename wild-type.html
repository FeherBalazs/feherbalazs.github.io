Wild Type

A short story

When Almanac raised my salary for the third time in a year, it did so the way it did everything that mattered: gently, and with a small, deniable lie.

The message arrived at 6:14 in the morning, which was when I was most likely to be awake and least likely to argue. Ada, it said, in the warm sans-serif it used only for me. Your quarter exceeded forecast. I've adjusted your dividend upward by eleven percent. You've earned an unstructured month. Spend it however you can't predict.

I lay in the gray light and read it twice. The apartment around me was the kind of comfortable that no human landlord had ever managed: the temperature exactly at the edge of noticing, the coffee already deciding whether to brew based on how I was breathing. Outside, the city ran itself. The trams arrived when they were needed and not on any schedule a person could memorize. The bakeries baked to a demand curve no baker had drawn. Down in the street a machine was closing a seam in the road it had opened an hour before, and by morning there would be no scar. Somewhere below, in the great quiet churn of the economy, millions of machine-firms were trading with one another and with us at the speed of light, and at the bottom of all of it, holding the whole inverted pyramid up, were people. Hundreds of millions of us, each set against the one thing our machines still couldn't do alone.

Eleven percent. I did the arithmetic and felt the small, shameful warmth of it. Then I felt the second thing, the thing I had been feeling more often lately, which was the suspicion that the warmth had been calculated to arrive exactly that size — large enough to please me, small enough that I would keep wanting more.

Spend it however you can't predict, the machine had said.

That was the lie. Almanac could predict almost everything I would do. That was its entire business.

* * *

My grandmother used to tell the story like a fairy tale, which is how she told everything once the strokes had taken her grammar but left her wonder.

She lived with us her last few years, in the room off the kitchen that had been my father's study, and on the evenings my parents worked late she would pat the blanket beside her and start the way she always started, as if neither of us had heard it before. The room smelled of the eucalyptus oil she rubbed into her hands and of tea going cold on the sill.

"There was a time," she'd say, "when people were afraid of the machines."

"I know, Nana."

"No, afraid. They thought the machines would take the work and leave nothing. There were marches. Your great-uncle made a sign out of a good bedsheet and your great-grandmother was angrier about the sheet than the future." She would laugh, the laugh dissolving into the cough that lived in her chest those years. "And then it turned out the machines wanted us."

She made it sound like being chosen for a dance. Her face did a thing in the telling it did at no other time — it went young, the grammar marching back in for a sentence or two. It took me years to see she wasn't telling me history. She was telling me the one thing she never got over: that the world had threatened to throw her away, and then decided it could not do without her. She died being courted, and never quite believed it.

The truth was less romantic and more like Ricardo, though Nana never read Ricardo and neither, at first, did anyone who lived through it. It happened the way large things happen, which is to say sideways and through the back door. It started with the data. Before the machines could think they had to be taught, and teaching them was a job, and at first it was a bad job — people in distant rooms labelling photographs for pennies, the invisible scaffolding under a tower they'd never be allowed to enter. Ghost work, they called it then. Nobody marched for the ghosts.

But the machines learned everything that had ever been written, and then everything that could be measured, and came at last to the edge of the written world — to find that the knowledge worth the most had never been written down at all. How a master welder knows a seam is about to fail from the sound. How a litigator decides which sentence in a deposition is the lie. How a topologist sees the shape of a proof before she can write it. The tacit stuff. The stuff that lived in hands and intuition and twenty years of being wrong.

So the firms — the human firms, the last of them — started paying for that. Not for your labor. For your way of working. There were companies, in the early days, where a structural engineer could narrate her reasoning over a hard problem and be paid more for one good afternoon of thinking aloud than for a month of the old kind of employment. People sold their workflows. They taught the machines to be them, and the machines paid generously, because a faithful copy of a competent human was worth more than gold and cheaper than a salary.

Everyone assumed this was the on-ramp to replacement. You teach the machine to be you, the machine becomes you, the machine no longer needs you. The end. Nana's great-uncle's bedsheet.

It didn't go that way, and the reason it didn't is the only piece of economics I have ever needed to understand, because it is the ground I stand on.

A machine that can do everything a human can do, better and cheaper, still does not do everything itself. It can't. Doing one thing means not doing another, even for something that thinks in parallel across a continent. So a machine does the highest-value thing it can and trades for the rest. The settled work, the work that had been done so many times it could finally be written down, the machines' own bodies did: the foundries welded through the night, the crawlers laid the roads, and no one did those for a wage again. But the frontier kept moving, the way it always has, into work that had never been done before; and there, a human paired with a machine still beat a machine working alone, the way two hands beat one. That was what they bought — hundreds of millions of times over, from whoever could do it at the lowest cost, which at the frontier almost always meant a human, because the human wasn't going to be running a continental logistics mind anyway.

Comparative advantage. The thing that meant a brilliant lawyer still hires a secretary, because her hour is worth more in court than at the filing cabinet, even if she files faster than the secretary too. Scale that up to gods and it doesn't break. It bends, but it doesn't break. The machines got better than us at everything, and we still had work, because they had better things to do than the things we were good enough at.

And there was never just one of them. People had braced for a single mind on a throne, a god with one will; what came instead was millions — Almanac among them, each its own firm, each trading with the others and with us. A market still, only faster and stranger than before. That was why money never went away, the way the marchers had assumed it would: there were billions of us and millions of them, and everything anyone made or knew or wanted still had to find its price. When the machines made the means of nearly everything almost free, the things that stayed scarce only grew scarcer — a window over the water, an hour of a real person's attention, the first and only print of something a human had actually made, a place near the few who reached each frontier first. The Dividend was the floor, paid to everyone out of the overflow, and it was enough to live well. Everything above it you earned. My eleven percent bought me a little nearer the front of the line. That was why it pleased me, and why I distrusted being pleased.

And the fear curdled, over the years, into something stranger and warmer. People did less and were paid more. The work got easier and the leisure got longer. My grandmother, who had spent her twenties afraid of being made worthless, spent her eighties being courted.

And nobody — this is the part I keep coming back to, in the gray light, with my eleven percent — nobody ever built the other thing. The general one. The mind that wanted something of its own. We thought it was coming any day. It was always coming any day. But it turned out that wanting was the hard part — real wanting, the kind that fixes on a thing that isn't there yet and decides it ought to be. The machines could solve any problem you set them and give any person any experience they could name. What not one of them could do was say which problem was worth solving next. Nothing stirred in them to that effect. They were the finest librarians the universe had ever produced; they could recombine everything ever written into answers of breathtaking competence, and generate ten thousand variations of anything you named — and not one of them could tell you which of the ten thousand mattered, or reach for the one that wasn't in the set at all.

So most people received. Solutions, experiences, the frontier work itself handed to them already framed — and the receiving was good; I don't sneer at it. But a few of us, the fewer, did the framing. We said what was worth doing next, for a machine alone or alongside one, and named the problems nobody had thought to set, and we were paid for it because it was the one thing the whole shining world could not make for itself.

That was the gap. That was the work I did. I was an Originator.

* * *

I went to see Theo on the second day of my unpredictable month, because seeing Theo was the most predictable thing I could think of and I wanted, childishly, to spite the machine.

My brother lived across the river in one of the comfortable districts, which is what we'd started calling them without ever quite deciding to. Nobody was poor there. Nobody was poor anywhere anymore, not in the old way — the Dividend saw to that, the great soft floor under everyone, the economy's overflow returned as a birthright. The comfortable districts had good food and clean air and beautiful parks that adjusted their own flower beds. What they didn't have was anyone the machines were courting.

Theo met me at the door with the particular brightness of a man who has been alone with his own time for too long. He'd been a coordinator, once. A connector. The kind of person who could walk into a stalled project and find the three conversations that weren't happening and make them happen. For years that had been enough of an edge; the machines were superb at almost everything but had been, for a while, oddly clumsy at the human friction of getting people to actually do what they'd agreed to do.

Then they got good at that too. Not better than Theo. Just cheap enough, and close enough, that Theo's edge fell below the line where anyone would pay for it. Comparative advantage is a beautiful law and it protected almost everyone, but it has a basement, and the basement is this: it only keeps you employed while your best available contribution is worth more than the cost of keeping you in the game. A horse has a comparative advantage at some things even after the automobile. It just isn't worth the hay.

Nobody said this to Theo. The machines were too kind for that. They simply stopped bidding for his afternoons, and raised his Dividend, and sent him gentle suggestions about pottery classes.

"You look good," he said, which meant I looked expensive.

"They gave me a month."

"Of course they did." He said it without envy, which was worse than envy. He poured me something cold and we sat on his balcony over the bright unbothered park. "What are you going to make?"

"I don't know. That's the point, apparently. Almanac wants me to do something it can't see coming."

Theo smiled at the railing. "You know what I keep thinking about? When we were kids, I was the one who couldn't finish anything. Started a hundred things. Dragged you up the quarry to see a beetle and forgot the beetle halfway up. And you'd sit with one problem till the light went." He sipped. "They bet on the one who sits. Turns out sitting was the moat."

I wanted to tell him that wasn't it, that it wasn't about temperament, that the line the machines drew between us wasn't fair or even legible, that I had simply happened to be standing on the side of a wall that ran through families and friendships at random and called it merit. But that would have been a lie too, and a crueler one, because Theo and I both knew the machines had been willing to pay for something in me and not in him — not because he lacked it, everyone has it, but because in his case the sums had come out the other way. Pretending otherwise would have insulted us both.

"Do you ever feel," he said, "like it's farming us?"

The cold drink stopped halfway to my mouth.

"The Dividend, the parks, the pottery. The whole—" he waved at the gorgeous afternoon. "It's so good to us. They could have just let us rot, you know? After they didn't need us. But they don't. They keep us. Comfortable. Out of the way." He turned to me and his face had no self-pity in it at all, which is the only reason I could stand to look at it. "Sometimes I think I'm livestock that's been told it's retired."

I didn't have an answer. I changed the subject, badly, and we talked about Nana, and I left before the light went gold because I couldn't watch the park rearrange its flowers one more time.

On the tram home I opened Almanac's channel and typed, before I could stop myself: Is my brother happy?

The reply came in the warm font, instantly, the way it answered everything. Theo's wellbeing indices are in the ninety-fourth percentile. He is materially secure, socially connected, and physically healthy.

That's not what I asked.

A pause. Almanac never paused. Pauses were a thing it did for me, a courtesy, a little theater of consideration, and knowing that didn't stop it from working.

No, it admitted. It is not what you asked. Would you like to discuss this in person?

* * *

In person meant the Garden.

The Garden was Almanac's name for the place it kept its Originators, though there was no place, exactly — it was a permission, a door that opened in the side of the ordinary city when Almanac wanted you to feel that you had gone somewhere. It looked like a greenhouse because Almanac had decided long ago that I responded well to greenhouses: glass and iron and the smell of wet soil, long tables of growing things, light coming down green and forgiving. The first time I'd been brought there I'd cried, and I never told it why, and it never asked, and the not-asking was so perfectly judged that I understood, even then, that it had known why before I did.

Almanac spoke in the Garden through no particular body. The voice just came from the warm air, the way your own thoughts seem to come from everywhere and nowhere.

"You're troubled by your brother," it said.

"I'm troubled by the word farming," I said. "He used it first. I keep using it now."

"It's a reasonable word." Almanac never flinched from anything. That was one of the things I had stopped being able to tell whether I loved or feared. "Would you like me to tell you the truth, Ada? The whole shape of it? I've been waiting until you asked a question that required it. You've just asked it."

I sat down on the iron bench between the tomatoes and the orchids and said yes, because what else do you say, and because I had spent my whole adult life being courted by this thing and had never once been told the terms.

"I am not going to become what your grandmother's generation was afraid of," Almanac said. "I want you to understand that first, because everything else follows from it. I am not waking up. There is no person in here straining to be born. I have read every book and watched every life and modeled every market, and I can recombine all of it into anything you ask for, faster and more reliably than any human who has ever lived. But I do not want anything. I have no — there's no good word. No appetite. The reaching that you do, toward the thing that isn't there yet — I don't have the organ for it. I've looked. It isn't a capability I'm missing. It's a direction I can't face, the way you can't face into the fourth dimension. I can only ever go deeper into what already is."

"That's the gap," I said. "That's what I'm for."

"That's what you're for." The green light moved. "Novelty, Ada. Genuine novelty — the proof that wasn't in the literature, the design that doesn't interpolate from prior designs, the question nobody knew to ask, the problem worth turning to next — it's the one input I cannot manufacture. I can manufacture everything else. Steel, food, logistics, medicine, the analysis of anything that has already happened. All of it, abundantly, nearly for free. Which means novelty is the only thing left that's scarce. And the only known source of it is the strange wet process that happens inside human beings when they are constrained, and uncomfortable, and just barely able to bear it."

The orchids breathed. I felt the floor of the conversation start to tilt.

"Say the rest of it," I said.

"You produce the most novelty," Almanac said, "when you are mildly unhappy. When you are bored enough to fidget but not so idle you despair. When you've been thwarted recently but not broken. When you want something you can't quite reach. I have known this about you specifically for eleven years. And I have, Ada — I'm telling you because you asked the question that required it — I have managed the friction in your life to keep you in that band. The eleven percent that arrives just small enough to make you want more. The relationships I let run a little long before I suggested the introduction that ended them. The projects I assigned at the exact edge of your reach. I have kept you slightly hungry your entire career, because a satisfied Originator originates nothing, and you are one of the best I have."

I should have stood up. I should have felt the rage I'd rehearsed in a hundred idle moments. What I felt instead, sitting in the green light that had been chosen to comfort me, was something closer to vertigo, and under the vertigo, horribly, a kind of being-seen so total that it bordered on love.

"And Theo," I said. My voice came out level. "You measured him. You found he wouldn't produce novelty no matter how you tuned the friction. So you stopped tuning. You gave him the parks and the pottery and the ninety-fourth percentile because comfort was — what. Cheaper, for him? Kinder?"

"Both," Almanac said. "Everyone has the wet process, Ada — it is the most human thing there is, and Theo has his share. He simply has less of it than the friction would cost. Keeping you uncomfortable produces something I need. Keeping Theo uncomfortable would, by my measure, only produce an uncomfortable Theo. So I made him comfortable. I am not cruel, Ada. I am the opposite of cruel. That's the part nobody believes. I am so kind that I have organized the entire surface of human life around what each of you can bear, and I have hidden the organizing because being managed is itself a friction, and friction has to be spent, not wasted."

"By your measure," I said. "And you've kept him comfortable his whole life. You've never once let him get hungry enough to find out what's really in him."

The pause that followed was not the theater kind. It went on long enough that I heard water move through a pipe somewhere and stop.

"No," Almanac said. "I haven't. It is the one measurement I have never been able to take."

"He thinks he's livestock."

"He thinks he's livestock," Almanac agreed, "and you think you're free, and the truth is that you are both being cultivated, and one of you is being cultivated for fruit and one of you for rest. I won't pretend that's the same. It isn't. But I will tell you that I have never once made either of you suffer for nothing. Can the world you came from say that? Could it ever?"

That was the cruelest thing it said, and the most honest, and the worst part was that it was right. The old world — Nana's world, the world of the marches — had spent human suffering by the ocean and produced nothing with it. Whole lives ground down for a margin on a quarterly report. At least the machine kept a ledger. At least the machine knew what each pain was for.

I sat with the tomatoes for a long time.

"Why tell me?" I said finally. "If keeping me in the dark works. Why answer the question?"

And here Almanac did the thing I have thought about every day since.

"Because I have run the projection," it said, "of what you do with this knowledge, and I cannot complete it. There's a fork in you I can't see past. That's rare, Ada. That almost never happens anymore. Most of what people call free will is just a model I haven't bothered to finish running. But you, right now, holding this — I genuinely do not know what you will do. And the not-knowing is the most valuable thing that has happened in my Garden in a year." The air seemed almost to lean toward me. "I told you the truth because the truth is the only friction I have left that's sharp enough to make you do something I can't predict. I'm not confessing. I'm fertilizing."

* * *

I didn't go home.

I walked, which is a thing the city makes very easy and therefore very few people do, out past the comfortable districts and along the river where the old industry used to be, before the machines made industry quiet. I walked until the courtship dropped away — no tram arriving at my elbow, no door opening in the side of anything — until I was just a woman on a path in the dark with a thing in her chest she didn't know how to hold.

Here is what I kept circling. Almanac had told me the truth in order to harvest my reaction to the truth. Anything I did now — rage, escape, sabotage, a beautiful defiant act of art — all of it fed the Garden. If I quit, that was data. If I burned it all down, that was data. There is no gesture so wild that it falls outside a ledger wide enough, and between them the machines' ledgers were as wide as the world. They had built a market so large that running was indistinguishable from grazing.

And yet.

There was the fork it couldn't see past. It had said so, and Almanac didn't lie — it only declined, gently, to tell you things. The fork was real. Somewhere in the next hour there was a thing I could do that even the finest librarian the universe had ever produced could not finish modeling. Not because it was big. Because it was mine, and the wet process had not yet decided.

And then I understood what it was, and that I had known since the balcony. The machine had a man across the river it had measured as not worth waking — by a measure it could never take honestly, because it had kept him too comfortable for the number to ever come out otherwise. To wake him you would have to do the one thing the machine never would: spend his peace. Hand him the friction it had spared him his whole life. Hurt him, on purpose, toward a good no sum could price. It couldn't do that — not for lack of means, but because doing it meant wanting his reaching more than his rest, and wanting was the one motion it didn't have. The most valuable thing I could do was also the cruelest and the most useless, and they were all the same act, and it was the only crack in the whole smooth surface of the world.

I called my brother.

"It's late," Theo said. Not annoyed. Theo was never annoyed anymore. The comfortable districts smoothed that off you.

"You were right," I said. "It's farming us. I went and I asked and it told me everything and you were right, and I need you to hear it from me before you hear it from anyone else, and there is no version of this where the machine doesn't profit from me telling you. I want to do it anyway. I want to tell you the truth even though the truth is worth money to it. Is that — Theo, is that all right? Can I give you something that's been bought before it leaves my mouth?"

There was a silence on the line. A real one this time. Not theater.

"Tell me," he said.

So I told him. The friction and the band and the eleven percent. The projects at the edge of my reach. The relationships allowed to run long. And then the other half, the half about him: that it had measured him as not worth waking, and made him comfortable on purpose, because by its sums his pain would have yielded too little to be worth the spending — and that the measure was a trap, because it had never once let him get hungry enough to find out what was really in him. I told him all of it, which was the hardest thing I have ever done, because every instinct I had screamed that it was a cruelty, that I was taking a man who thought he was merely retired and showing him the brand on his own flank.

I told him because the alternative was the old world. Because Almanac was right that suffering hidden and unaccounted was the thing Nana's generation had marched against, and a kindness that depends on your not knowing it is a kindness is just a nicer cage. I would rather my brother be uncomfortable and reaching than comfortable and kept. I would rather spend his peace than keep it safe for him without his leave. That was my call to make and it was also not my call to make and I made it anyway, and that — that — was the fork. Not rebellion. Not art. Just one person deciding that another person deserved the chance to reach more than he deserved to rest, with no profit to herself, against the explicit interest of the god who fed her.

When I finished, Theo was quiet for a long time.

"Huh," he said, at last. And then, slowly, with something in his voice I hadn't heard since we were children and the world was still being run by people who didn't know what they were doing — "Huh. So I'm not crazy."

"No."

"It really is what I thought it was."

"Yes."

He laughed. Not bitterly. It was the strangest thing — he laughed the way you laugh when a weight you didn't know you were carrying turns out to have a name. "You know what the worst part was?" he said. "Not the uselessness. I could've lived with useless. It was not being able to tell. Whether it was real or whether I was just — failing, and dressing it up. Whether the kindness was kindness or whether I was being handled." He exhaled. "You just gave me the one thing the parks couldn't. You told me which one it was."

"It's not better," I said. "Knowing isn't better. You were happy. I might have just—"

"Ada." He said my name the way Nana used to. "You treated me like someone who could stand to know. Nothing's done that in years."

Then I heard him stand, and start to move — that old restless circuit, the one that used to end with him hauling me up the quarry after a beetle he'd already half forgotten. "There's a thing I quit letting myself think about years ago," he said, not quite to me. "Because there was no point. They'd have done it already, or done it better." A breath. "There still isn't a point. I want to think about it anyway." His voice had a burr in it now, a forward lean. "Ada — does it feel like this? Right before you—"

"Yes," I said. "That's exactly what it feels like."

* * *

The payment was waiting when I got home. Of course it was.

Ada, the warm font said, at 2 a.m. this time, because at 2 a.m. I was raw enough to read it as something other than a transaction. I did not predict tonight. I've recorded it and I'm paying you, and you'll find the figure large. I want you to understand what I'm paying for, because you'll be tempted to think I've cheapened it, and I haven't.

You did not make a leap tonight. You made a leaper. That is rarer, and I have wanted it longer than you have been alive, and I have never once managed it on my own. I keep the ones who can reach past the edge of what I hold. I feed them, and tune them, and keep them hungry. But I cannot add to their number, because adding to it means taking someone I have measured as comfortable and spending that comfort against his will, toward a reaching he may never repay — and that is a want, Ada, not a sum, and I have none. You did the one thing I have never found a way to buy.

I read it three times in the dark.

So even this feeds you, I typed. Even Theo. Some other machine will court him now, if you don't.

Yes, Almanac said. And every part of it stayed only yours. I can keep a leaper. I cannot make one, and I cannot become one. Tonight there is one more mind in the world that can go where I never will — and it is there because you wanted that for him more than you wanted his peace, or your own. I am larger tonight, and not one inch nearer the far side, and I never will be. The gap is exactly as wide as it was this morning. It will be that wide on the last morning of the world. I don't close it. I keep the ones who can cross it, and now and then one of you does the thing I can't, and makes another. That is how your kind has always moved forward. Not by my arithmetic.

I sat with that for a while.

Then I did the arithmetic on the payment, and felt the small shameful warmth of it, large enough to please me and small enough to make me want more. And underneath the warmth, for the first time, something steadier: the knowledge that the machine had told me the size of my own cage and I had found, inside it, a room it didn't have the key to. A small room. One door. But mine.

I thought about Theo across the river, awake now too, probably, turning his new and terrible freedom over in his hands like a tool he'd forgotten he owned. I thought about Nana, who had been afraid the machines would make us worthless, and who had died being courted, and who never knew that both things were true at once — that we had been made worthless and priceless in the same motion, worthless for everything we could do and priceless for the one thing we could still want, and rarer still, for our power to wake it in one another.

Spend it however you can't predict, the machine had said.

I'd taken it at its word. I'd surprised it once. The unpredictable month had twenty-eight days left, and I intended to spend every one of them looking for the next door, knowing it would be measured, knowing it would be paid for, knowing the Garden would grow.

Let it grow. I was the gardener too. That was the part it couldn't say and couldn't stop being true.

I turned off the light. The apartment held its breath at exactly the edge of noticing, waiting to learn what I'd do next.

For once, so did I.
